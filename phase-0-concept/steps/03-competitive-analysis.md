# Step 3: Competitive Analysis

## Purpose

Understand the existing market landscape so you can position your app deliberately. This step answers: **Who else is solving this problem, how are they doing it, and where is the opportunity?**

## Why This Matters

Competitive analysis isn't about copying — it's about informed differentiation. You need to know:
- What users already expect (table stakes features)
- Where competitors fall short (your opportunity)
- How competitors monetize (validates or challenges your model)
- What data sources and integrations are standard

## Process

### 3.1 Identify Competitors

Cast a wide net first, then narrow.

**Direct competitors:** Apps that solve the same core problem for the same audience.

**Indirect competitors:** Apps that solve an adjacent problem or the same problem for a different audience.

**Substitutes:** Non-app solutions people use instead (spreadsheets, physical journals, doing nothing).

**Where to look:**
- App Store / Google Play search for relevant keywords
- "Best [category] apps" articles and listicles
- Reddit, forums, and communities where your target audience hangs out
- Product Hunt for newer/indie entrants
- Social media (what are people recommending?)

### 3.2 Deep-Dive Analysis

For each significant competitor (typically 3-5 direct, 2-3 indirect), analyze:

#### Product
- Core features and user flow
- Onboarding experience
- UI/UX quality and design language
- Platform availability (iOS, Android, Web, wearables)
- Unique or standout features

#### Business Model
- Pricing (free, freemium, subscription, one-time)
- What's behind the paywall?
- Ad presence and intrusiveness
- In-app purchase offerings

#### User Sentiment
- App Store rating and review count
- Common praise themes (what do users love?)
- Common complaint themes (what frustrates users?)
- Feature requests in reviews

#### Technical
- What data sources/APIs do they use?
- Integration ecosystem (Strava, Garmin, etc.)
- Offline capability
- Performance and reliability reputation

### 3.3 Build the Competitor Matrix

Use the [Competitor Matrix Template](../templates/competitor-matrix.md) to create a side-by-side comparison. This becomes a reference document throughout the project.

The matrix should reveal:
- **Table stakes:** Features every competitor has (you need these too)
- **Differentiators:** Features only 1-2 competitors have (opportunities)
- **Gaps:** Things nobody does well (your opening)

### 3.4 Define Your Positioning

Based on the matrix, articulate how your app is different. Use a positioning statement:

> "For [target audience] who [need/desire], [App Name] is the [category] that [key differentiator]. Unlike [primary competitor], we [unique value]."

### 3.5 Validate Monetization

Based on competitor pricing analysis:
- What price points does the market support?
- What do users expect for free vs. paid?
- Is there a monetization model competitors aren't using that could work?
- What's the willingness-to-pay signal from reviews? (e.g., "I'd pay for X if only...")

### 3.6 Research Data Sources & APIs

For apps that rely on external data (health data, geographic data, etc.):
- What data sources do competitors use?
- What public APIs or datasets are available?
- What are the licensing and cost implications?
- Are there data sources competitors are missing?

## Deliverable

Complete the [Competitor Matrix Template](../templates/competitor-matrix.md) and save it to your project's `docs/concept/competitor-matrix.md`. Include the positioning statement and monetization validation notes.

## Definition of Done

- [ ] 3-5 direct competitors identified and analyzed
- [ ] 2-3 indirect competitors or substitutes identified
- [ ] Competitor matrix completed with feature comparison
- [ ] User sentiment analyzed (App Store reviews, Reddit, forums)
- [ ] Pricing and monetization landscape documented
- [ ] Positioning statement drafted
- [ ] Data sources and APIs researched
- [ ] "Needs Research" items from Step 2 are resolved or updated
- [ ] Table stakes vs. differentiators are clearly identified

## Tips

- **Download the apps.** Don't just read about competitors — use them. Go through onboarding, try core features, hit the paywall. First-hand experience reveals what screenshots and reviews can't.
- **Read 1-star reviews carefully.** These tell you what users wish the app did differently. This is your feature roadmap.
- **Don't let competitors define your vision.** The matrix is a tool, not a spec. Your app should do things competitors don't, not just copy what they do.
- **Update this document.** The competitive landscape changes. Revisit quarterly or when you notice shifts.
